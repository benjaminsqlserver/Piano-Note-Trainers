What does "flat" mean?

A flat (♭) lowers a note's pitch by one semitone — the smallest step used in Western music, equal to the distance from any piano key to the very next key down, black or white. Written after a letter name, "C♭" means "the note C, lowered one semitone." On a keyboard that usually lands you on a black key, since most white keys have a black key sitting right below them.

Every one of the 7 natural letters — A, B, C, D, E, F, G — can be flattened, giving the 7 flat keys this app teaches: A♭, B♭, C♭, D♭, E♭, F♭, G♭.

Two of the natural letter pairs — B/C and E/F — sit only a semitone apart already, with no black key between them. So flattening C or F doesn't reach a black key at all: it lands squarely on the previous white key. That's why C♭ sounds identical to B, and F♭ sounds identical to E. Two different names, the exact same piano key — musicians call this an enharmonic equivalent.
