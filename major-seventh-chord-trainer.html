How a major 7th chord is built

A major 7th chord is a 4-note chord — a major triad with one more note stacked on top. Every major 7th chord — in any of the 12 keys — is built the exact same way, by counting semitones to the right from a root note:

  1. Start on the root — the note that names the chord.
  2. Count 4 semitones to the right to land on the major 3rd.
  3. From there, count 3 more semitones to the right (7 semitones from the root) to land on the perfect 5th.
  4. From there, count 4 more semitones to the right (11 semitones from the root) to land on the major 7th.
Example — D major 7th (Dmaj7): start on D, count 4 semitones right to reach F♯, 3 more to reach A, then 4 more to reach C♯. D‑F♯‑A‑C♯ is the Dmaj7 chord. The pattern 4, 3, 4 (11 semitones total from root to top) is what makes a chord "major 7th" — every one of the 12 major 7th chords is this same shape, just moved to start on a different key.
Compare this to a dominant 7th chord, which counts 4, 3, 3 instead — a major 7th chord raises that ♭7th by one more semitone, landing just one semitone below the octave rather than two. That single semitone changes everything: instead of the dominant 7th's restless pull toward another chord, a major 7th chord sounds lush, dreamy, and content to stay right where it is — the signature color of jazz ballads and soft gospel tonic chords, and the chord a major scale naturally builds on its own 1st and 4th degrees (Imaj7 and IVmaj7).

This MIDI exercise plays all 12 major 7th chords, one after another, moving around the circle of fourths — each chord's root sits a perfect 4th (5 semitones) above the last one: C → F → B♭ → E♭ → A♭ → D♭ → G♭/F♯ → B → E → A → D → G → back to C. Every chord is played as a full 4-note major 7th (root, 3rd, 5th, and major 7th together).
