How a 6th chord is built

A 6th chord is a 4-note chord — a major triad with one more note stacked on top. Unlike every chord in Lessons 13-17, that extra note is a 6th, not a 7th. Every 6th chord — in any of the 12 keys — is built the exact same way, by counting semitones to the right from a root note:

  1. Start on the root — the note that names the chord.
  2. Count 4 semitones to the right to land on the major 3rd.
  3. From there, count 3 more semitones to the right (7 semitones from the root) to land on the perfect 5th.
  4. From there, count 2 more semitones to the right (9 semitones from the root) to land on the major 6th.
Example — D 6th (D6): start on D, count 4 semitones right to reach F♯, 3 more to reach A, then 2 more to reach B. D‑F♯‑A‑B is the D6 chord. The pattern 4, 3, 2 (9 semitones total from root to top) is what makes a chord a "6th chord" — every one of the 12 6th chords is this same shape, just moved to start on a different key.
A 6th chord never pulls anywhere the way a 7th chord does — there's no ♭7th or major 7th creating tension that wants to resolve. That's exactly why jazz and gospel pianists reach for it as a warm, "at rest" color, most famously as the very last chord of a tune (I6) in place of a plain triad or a major 7th. Watch out for one mix-up: a 6th chord and a minor 7th chord built a major 6th below it share the exact same four notes — C6 (C‑E‑G‑A) is exactly the same pitch classes as Am7 (A‑C‑E‑G), just voiced from a different root. Which chord it "is" depends entirely on which note is functioning as the root in context.

This MIDI exercise plays all 12 6th chords, one after another, moving around the circle of fourths — each chord's root sits a perfect 4th (5 semitones) above the last one: C → F → B♭ → E♭ → A♭ → D♭ → G♭/F♯ → B → E → A → D → G → back to C. Every chord is played as a full 4-note 6th chord (root, 3rd, 5th, and 6th together).
